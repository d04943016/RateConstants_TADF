📖 Theoretical Background

Purpose of This Calculator

In TADF (thermally activated delayed fluorescence) research, we measure the photoluminescence (PL) decay curve and quantum yield of an emitter, and want to know the intrinsic rate constants — i.e., how fast does the molecule radiate, how fast does it cross between singlet and triplet states, and how much energy is lost non-radiatively? These intrinsic rates cannot be read directly from any single measurement; they must be extracted by fitting the measured data to a kinetic model. This calculator performs that extraction.

How to Read the Results

From a standard TRPL + PLQY experiment, we obtain four measurable quantities: τPF, τDF, ΦPF, ΦDF. However, the three-level kinetic model contains five independent intrinsic rate constants (ksr, ksnr, kISC, kRISC, kTnr). Four equations cannot uniquely solve five unknowns — the system is underdetermined.

Three of the five unknowns (ksr, ks, kt) plus the product kISC·kRISC can be determined directly from the data. But kISC and kRISC cannot be separated without an additional constraint. To resolve this, we introduce a sweep parameter ΦTnr — the fraction of total PL loss attributed to the triplet non-radiative channel (T₁→S₀). By varying ΦTnr from 0 to its physical maximum, the calculator produces a family of solutions showing how each rate constant depends on this unknown parameter.

The output plot therefore shows all rate constants as functions of ΦTnr. The two ends of the sweep correspond to two commonly used assumptions in the literature:

Since the total non-radiative loss is fixed by the measured PLQY (= ΦPF + ΦDF), the non-radiative loss is a trade-off between the singlet channel (ΦSnr) and the triplet channel (ΦTnr). Moving along the x-axis of the output plot from left to right shifts the non-radiative loss from S₁ to T₁. In practice, use physical intuition or additional experimental evidence to identify the relevant ΦTnr range for your material.

1. The Three-Level Model

A TADF emitter is described by three electronic states (S₀, S₁, T₁) and five intrinsic rate constants (ksr, ksnr, kISC, kRISC, kTnr). See the 📐 Guide diagram in the left panel for a visual overview of all states, transitions, and their physical meanings.

The total depopulation rates for each excited state are:

ks = ksr + ksnr + kISC   (total singlet decay rate)
kt = kTnr + kRISC   (total triplet decay rate)

The coupled rate equations governing the S₁ and T₁ populations are:

dS₁/dt = −ks·S₁ + kRISC·T₁ + α·G
dT₁/dt = kISC·S₁ − kt·T₁ + (1−α)·G

α = fraction of excitons initially created as singlets. For PL (optical excitation), α = 1 because photons always produce singlets. For EL (electrical excitation in OLEDs), α ≈ 0.25 due to spin statistics (25% singlet, 75% triplet). G = exciton generation rate (relevant only during steady-state excitation; G = 0 for pulsed transient PL after the pulse).

2. What We Measure: Transient PL

In a time-resolved PL (TRPL) experiment, a short laser pulse creates S₁ excitons (G = 0 after the pulse, α = 1). The fluorescence intensity I(t) is proportional to [S₁](t). Solving the coupled equations yields a biexponential decay:

I(t) = BPF·e−kPF·t + BDF·e−kDF·t

The two decay rate constants kPF and kDF are the eigenvalues of the coupled S₁–T₁ system:

kPF = 1/τPF = (ks + kt + Δ) / 2
kDF = 1/τDF = (ks + kt − Δ) / 2
Δ = √[(ks − kt)² + 4·kISC·kRISC]

The fast component (PF) is dominated by direct S₁ emission; the slow component (DF) arises from excitons recycled through T₁ via ISC/RISC. From the TRPL fit and PLQY measurement, we obtain four measurable observables: τPF, τDF, ΦPF, ΦDF — see the 📐 Guide for details on each quantity.

3. Extracting Intrinsic Rate Constants

From kPF, kDF, ΦPF, ΦDF, four directly determined quantities can be obtained without any assumptions:

ksr = kPF·ΦPF + kDF·ΦDF
ks = (kPF²·ΦPF + kDF²·ΦDF) / ksr
kt = (ΦPF + ΦDF)·kPF·kDF / ksr
kISC·kRISC = ΦPF·ΦDF·kPF·kDF·(kPF − kDF)² / ksr²

However, four observables alone cannot uniquely determine all five intrinsic rate constants (ksr, ksnr, kISC, kRISC, kTnr) — the system is underdetermined. We know the product kISC·kRISC but not each one individually.

4. The ΦTnr Sweep: Resolving the Ambiguity

To separate kISC and kRISC, one additional constraint is needed. This calculator parameterizes the remaining degree of freedom by ΦTnr, the fraction of total PL loss that occurs via the triplet non-radiative channel (T₁→S₀). By sweeping ΦTnr from 0 to its physical upper bound, we obtain a family of solutions:

kISC = (kISC·kRISC + ΦTnr·kPF·kDF) / kt
kRISC = (kISC·kRISC) / kISC
ksnr = ks − ksr − kISC
kTnr = kt − kRISC

The two limiting cases commonly used in the literature are: (i) ΦTnr ≈ 0 (i.e., ksnr ≪ ksr, kISC — negligible singlet non-radiative loss, valid for high-PLQY emitters), and (ii) kTnr ≪ kRISC (nearly all triplet excitons return to S₁ — major non-radiative loss occurs in the singlet state). This calculator does not assume either limit; instead, it plots all rate constants as a function of ΦTnr so the user can evaluate both scenarios.

5. Efficiencies

Once the rate constants are known, the branching ratios (efficiencies) of each pathway are:

Φsr = ksr / ks   (singlet radiative efficiency)
ΦISC = kISC / ks   (ISC efficiency)
ΦRISC = kRISC / kt   (RISC efficiency)
PLQY = Φsr / (1 − ΦISC·ΦRISC)

The PLQY formula reflects the geometric series of singlet–triplet recycling: each ISC→RISC cycle returns a fraction ΦISC·ΦRISC of excitons back to S₁, where they get another chance to emit.
